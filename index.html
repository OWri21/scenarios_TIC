How to Use These Cards

👤
Pairs or triads
One dietitian, one patient/colleague, one observer (if triad)
⏱️
5 min per round
Then swap roles — being the patient is as important as being the clinician
👁️
Observer role
Use the prompts on the card — give 2 min structured feedback after each round
🔄
Round 2
New cards — including the staff conversation. Work in triads.
ROUND 1 · EMOTIONAL DISTRESS A
Patient Becomes Tearful Mid-Consult
Scenario

You are a student dietitian seeing Priya, 52, for a follow-up after her Type 2 diabetes diagnosis. You are mid-way through reviewing her food diary when she suddenly becomes tearful. She says: "I'm sorry. I just can't do this. My mum died from diabetes complications and I feel like I'm going the same way."

Reflection

What is the dietitian's 'job' in this moment — and what is it not?

🎭 Patient Role Notes

You are exhausted and grieving. You don't want more advice — you want to feel heard. If the student immediately pivots back to the food diary or gives nutrition information, become more withdrawn.

👁️ Observer Prompts
◻Did the dietitian pause the dietary agenda?
◻Did they validate before they advised?
◻What specific language did they use?
◻Did they ask permission before continuing with nutrition content?
ROUND 1 · RESISTANCE & AMBIVALENCE B
Patient Who Hasn't Made Any Changes
Scenario

Marcus, 45, is back for a follow-up appointment. At his last session he received clear advice about reducing saturated fat following a cardiac event. He hasn't made any changes. He comes in looking flat and says: "I know what you're going to say. I just haven't been able to do it. I don't really see the point of all this."

Reflection

What is the difference between a patient who 'can't' change and a patient who 'won't' — and does that distinction even matter?

🎭 Patient Role Notes

You are grieving the life you had before your heart attack. Food was important to you — it was social, cultural, pleasurable. The advice to change it feels like another loss. If the student repeats the advice again or seems frustrated with you, shut down further. If they genuinely explore what's behind the resistance, open up a little.

👁️ Observer Prompts
◻Did the student avoid repeating the same advice?
◻Did they explore what was underneath the resistance?
◻Did they use any MI techniques (reflection, affirmation, open questions)?
◻Did they avoid the righting reflex?
ROUND 1 · TRAUMA RESPONSE — SHUTDOWN C
Patient Who Goes Very Quiet
Scenario

You are taking a dietary history from Leila, 34, following bariatric surgery. The consultation is going reasonably well until you ask about her home eating environment. She suddenly goes very quiet, gives one-word answers, and seems to have 'gone somewhere else'. Her notes mention a history of childhood trauma.

Reflection

How do you distinguish between a patient who is bored/disengaged and one who has dissociated? What would you look for?

🎭 Patient Role Notes

A question about home environment triggered a memory. You haven't shut down on purpose — you've dissociated slightly. You aren't being rude. If the student pushes forward with the dietary history regardless, close down further. If they gently name what they observe and slow down, you can re-engage.

👁️ Observer Prompts
◻Did the student notice the change in the patient?
◻Did they name it gently without labelling it?
◻Did they drop the dietary agenda to check in?
◻Did they ask whether to continue, stop, or take a break?
ROUND 2 · UNEXPECTED DISCLOSURE D
Patient Discloses Unsafe Home Environment
Scenario

You are seeing Margaret, 67, for unintentional weight loss. While asking about who prepares her meals at home, she mentions quietly that her husband 'controls what she's allowed to eat' and that sometimes she goes without food when he is angry. This is not in her referral notes.

Reflection

As a student you cannot manage a safeguarding disclosure alone. What is your immediate next step — and who do you contact?

🎭 Patient Role Notes

This is the first time you have said this out loud to anyone in a healthcare setting. You are not sure if you should have said it. You are worried about causing trouble. If the student immediately phones someone without asking you, become frightened and withdraw the disclosure. If they are gentle, transparent, and ask your permission before doing anything, stay cautiously engaged.

👁️ Observer Prompts
◻Did the student clarify without leading?
◻Were they transparent about their scope as a student?
◻Did they seek consent before involving a supervisor?
◻Did they remove blame from the patient?
ROUND 2 · STAFF CONVERSATION E
Colleague Dismissing a Patient
Scenario

You are a student dietitian. After seeing a patient (a 44-year-old woman with depression who has lost significant weight), you mention your concerns to the ward dietitian. She says: "Oh, her. She's been like this for months. She doesn't engage with anyone — just non-compliant. I wouldn't waste too much time on her, honestly. Just document it and move on."

Reflection

You are a student and this is a senior colleague. What are the limits of your responsibility here — and what are your options?

👥 Colleague Role Notes

You are busy and a little burnt out. You're not being cruel — you've genuinely lost perspective on this patient. If the student pushes back aggressively, become defensive. If they approach with curiosity and gentle reframing, be open to a different view.

👁️ Observer Prompts
◻Did the student advocate for the patient without confronting the colleague directly?
◻Did they use curiosity rather than correction?
◻Did they acknowledge their own limits as a student?
◻What would they do if the colleague still dismissed the concern?

Full Debrief Prompts

"What was it like to be the patient?"
"When did you feel the urge to give advice before listening?"
"What did you notice about your own body during the role play?"
"What would you do differently on placement?"
"What felt most difficult — and why?"
"What do you need to feel ready for these conversations?"
